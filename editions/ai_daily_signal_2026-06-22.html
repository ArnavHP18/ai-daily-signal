Day 11: the flagships stay dark — and the legal ground under the ban is cracking

Fable 5 and Mythos 5 remain disabled worldwide into Monday — Day 11 since the Jun 12 Commerce directive barring foreign-national access. The fresh signal this scan is not a restoration but a shift in terrain: legal scholars and former Commerce officials increasingly argue the directive's foundation is shaky. Bloomberg reports Lutnick's order "claims new power over AI models"; Gizmodo and Just Security note the letter leans on EAR §744.22 — a rule about restricting the "export" of "items" — and that letting a foreign national query a hosted chatbot may not be an "export" at all, with one former senior Commerce adviser calling the letter "so badly drafted it might not restrict API/chatbot access." Anthropic, already litigating a related designation in two federal courts, keeps complying while disputing the order; Trump's Sunday "not now" softening still stands, but the directive is not lifted. Markets still price restoration — Kalshi ~57% before Jul 1, ~67% before Jul 10, ~75% before Jul 17 (Kalshi). Allies stay locked out; the ban "further strains alliances" (Al Jazeera).

So what: the fight is migrating from press statements to legal argument — a shaky basis raises the odds of reversal, but also of a drawn-out court battle that outlasts the market-implied July window. Keep the fallback live and the migration priced; do not bank on a clean July switch-back.

Where it stands. As of this Monday scan, both Fable 5 and Mythos 5 are still offline for all users worldwide — Day 11 of the blackout; every other Claude model remains available, with traffic routed to Opus 4.8. Anthropic continues to dispute the directive while complying, and says it is working to restore access "as soon as possible" (Anthropic, Fortune).

The new note: the legal foundation is under fire. The Jun 12 letter, signed by Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick under the Bureau of Industry and Security, invokes EAR §744.22 — which lets BIS restrict the "export" of "items" at risk of diversion to a "military-intelligence end use." Multiple analyses now question whether that even reaches a hosted chatbot or API. Bloomberg frames it as Lutnick "claiming new power over AI models"; Gizmodo reports the basis "looks increasingly shaky," and a former senior Commerce policy adviser argues the letter "is so badly drafted it might not restrict API/chatbot access at all" — there is arguably "no export here to restrict" (Just Security, Volkov Law).

The deal track — and the litigation track. Anthropic's technical staff have met with Commerce nearly daily and Lutnick has held regular calls as he "works toward a deal"; the government is seeking assurances the models cannot be turned against the US (Globe and Mail). In parallel, Anthropic is already litigating a related designation in two federal courts (Northern District of California and the D.C. Circuit), challenging it as unlawful retaliation. No signed agreement and no restoration date have been confirmed — markets remain the most concrete read (Kalshi ~57% by Jul 1, ~67% by Jul 10, ~75% by Jul 17; Kalshi).

The political frame. Sunday's signal was rhetorical relief: asked by The Axios Show whether he still viewed Anthropic as a security threat, Trump said "not now, but a week ago, maybe," crediting "quick" and "responsible" compliance. The directive sits atop the Jun 2 executive order "Promoting Advanced AI Innovation and Security," which set up a secure-deployment framework with up to 30 days of voluntary government early access. The on-record rationale remains Lutnick's fear of diversion to military-intelligence users in China or Russia.

The competitive cost. While the flagships sit dark, the gap is being filled by others: open-weights labs shipped fast (today's #4), and allies remain cut off — the EU Commission has warned the curbs "should not be discriminatory" (Euronews). Anthropic spent the prior week opening a Seoul office and signing Samsung/LG/NAVER deployments in a market it cannot fully serve (today's #6) — and is racing toward its own IPO (today's #8) under the shadow of the ban.

Watch next. Whether the shaky legal basis converts into a court challenge or a negotiated climbdown; whether restoration arrives clean or narrowed to nationality gating; whether a signed deal is announced inside the Jul 1 market window; and whether any parallel directive touches OpenAI or Google.

IMPACT 6/10#9 POLICY · EU

EU clock: 41 days to Aug 2 transparency + GPAI enforcement

Near steady state, calendar ticking. From Aug 2 the AI Act brings Article 50 transparency duties (disclosure when users interact with AI; labelling of synthetic media) plus the start of the Commission's GPAI enforcement powers — documentation/information requests, model evaluations, compliance/risk-mitigation measures, and fines up to the greater of €15M or 3% of global turnover (Latham, artificialintelligenceact.eu). The GPAI Code of Practice (transparency, copyright, safety & security) is the practical compliance tool; signing carries a "presumption of conformity." The safety chapter binds only the ~5–15 systemic-risk providers above 10^25 FLOP; ~24 firms (Anthropic, Google, Microsoft, Mistral, OpenAI and others) have signed. A separate Digital Omnibus postponement of some high-risk obligations remains provisionally agreed, not yet formally adopted — Aug 2 transparency and GPAI enforcement still stand.

The Aug 2 enforcement switch is 41 days out — if your synthetic-media labelling and Article 50 transparency workstreams aren't staffed, they're late.
