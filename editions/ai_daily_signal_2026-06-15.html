Day 4: Anthropic's best models stay dark — Amazon is named, defenders revolt, and execs fly to D.C.

Fable 5 and Mythos 5 remain disabled worldwide into Monday, four days after the Jun 12 (5:21pm ET) US Commerce directive barring foreign-national access. The weekend filled in the rest of the story. The trigger is now named: Axios and Fortune report Amazon (CEO Andy Jassy) called the White House Thursday night with a report showing its researchers had jailbroken Fable 5 to reach Mythos's cyber capabilities — and that at least five other companies phoned senior officials before the order landed Friday. Now the pushback is organized: a 40+-signatory letter led by Alex Stamos urges President Trump to reverse the ban, and Anthropic has flown senior technical staff to Washington to brief officials and float model changes that could reopen access. Still no restoration date.

So what: this has hardened from a one-off into a precedent with a named accuser, an industry revolt, and live negotiation — but no clock. Treat the outage as open-ended: keep your fallback live on your top-tier workloads and assume days-to-weeks, not hours.

Where it stands. As of this Monday scan, both Fable 5 and Mythos 5 are still offline for all users worldwide; every other Claude model remains available, with traffic routed to Opus 4.8. Anthropic has not announced a restoration date and continues to dispute the directive while complying (Fortune, Anthropic).

Who pulled the trigger. Per Axios and Fortune, Amazon — a major Anthropic investor and infrastructure partner — called administration officials Thursday night to share a report in which Amazon researchers used a prompt chain to get Fable 5 to surface cyberattack-relevant information that was meant to be off-limits. Calls from Amazon plus at least five other companies to senior officials Thursday evening and Friday morning preceded the Friday-night shutdown. Separately, Semafor reported the move was also linked to concern that a China-linked group had accessed Mythos.

The defenders' revolt. A loosely organized group of more than 40 security leaders — led by former Facebook CSO Alex Stamos and including Luta Security's Katie Moussouris, SocialProof's Rachel Tobac, Veracode co-founder Chris Wysopal, Paul Vixie, Sophos CEO Joe Levy and Nvidia's Aaron Grattafiori, plus CISOs at firms such as Adobe and Zoom — sent a letter urging the administration to restore access, arguing the issue exists across other leading models too. Their warning: the order "has taken the best models away from defenders, created market uncertainty, and risked America's AI leadership without any real risk to justify it" (Axios).

The negotiation. Anthropic has flown senior technical staff to D.C., after virtual meetings with White House officials over the weekend, to explain the models' safety measures and discuss changes that could address the government's concerns and potentially allow reinstatement (Axios). The company maintains it has seen only verbal evidence of a narrow, non-universal jailbreak, that comparable capability exists in rival public models (it has named OpenAI's GPT-5.5), and that recalling a model at this scale on that basis would, if generalized, "halt all new model deployments for all frontier model providers."

Watch next. Whether the order narrows from a global shutoff to nationality gating; whether the D.C. talks yield a restoration date; whether any China-access claim is substantiated; whether a parallel directive touches OpenAI or Google; and whether Congress schedules oversight. Trump's Jun 2 AI executive order remains the backdrop. Analysts are already treating the episode as a case study in the absence of a government AI-safety playbook (Just Security).
