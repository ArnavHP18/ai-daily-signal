The fourth US frontier lab finally enters the coding market — Meta ships a paid agent

On Jul 9, Meta Superintelligence Labs released Muse Spark 1.1, a multimodal reasoning model built for agentic coding, into public preview on the Meta Model API for US developers — and, notably, as Meta's first paid agent model, priced at $1.25 / $4.25 per million input/output tokens (TechCrunch, CNBC, Meta). The model can write and debug code, use software and external tools, understand text/images/video, and run multi-step tasks with less human intervention; Meta says it leads the tool-use and tool-augmented-reasoning rows and places third on coding and multimodal (MarkTechPost, Fortune). It arrives the same day OpenAI's GPT-5.6 went fully GA (Developing) — the culmination of a week in which all four US frontier labs shipped.

So what: Meta is charging money to attack the exact category — agentic coding — that is powering Anthropic's ~$47B run-rate. A fourth credible, metered coding model resets the buyer's shortlist (Claude Code / GPT-5.6-Codex / Gemini / now Muse Spark) and hands developers real pricing leverage. But it is US-only public preview, not global GA, and ranks third on raw coding — evaluate it as a price/tool-use option, not yet a Claude replacement.

What happened. Meta Superintelligence Labs (under Alexandr Wang) launched Muse Spark 1.1 on Jul 9 in public preview on the Meta Model API, restricted for now to developers in the United States. The first version of Muse Spark was announced in April; 1.1 is positioned as a multimodal, tool-using agentic model that can manage digital workflows and deploy features in enterprise systems. Crucially, it is Meta's first model with a public per-token price — $1.25 in / $4.25 out per million tokens — a break from Meta's open-weight Llama posture and a direct move into the paid API market Anthropic and OpenAI dominate.

Why it's the lead. This is the biggest genuinely new item on the wire and a structural shift: the one major US lab that had ceded frontier coding is now selling into it. Agentic coding is the single fastest-growing software category of 2025–26 and the engine of Anthropic's revenue; a fourth metered entrant widens the competitive field, pressures pricing, and gives enterprises another vendor to pit against Claude Code and GPT-5.6-Codex. It also partially answers a thread this feed has tracked for weeks (the "will Meta ship a frontier model / Watermelon" question) — Meta shipped something real and charged for it.

The caveats — read them. It is US-only public preview, not global GA. On Meta's own scorecard it leads tool-use but is only third on coding and multimodal — i.e., strong for agent orchestration, not (yet) the top raw coder. Benchmarks are self-reported at launch; independent evals (SWE-Bench, Terminal-Bench) have not landed. And this is "1.1," an incremental release atop April's Spark 1.0, not a from-scratch flagship — the rumored higher-compute "Watermelon" frontier model remains unconfirmed and unshipped.

Watch next. Independent coding/agent benchmarks vs Claude, GPT-5.6 and GLM-5.2; whether the preview opens beyond the US and to enterprise tiers; how aggressively Meta prices against Anthropic's metered Fable 5 (Deadline Radar, Jul 12) and Together AI's open-inference economics (New); and whether "Watermelon" ever materialises above Spark.

IMPACT 5/10#2 MODELS · OPENAI

OpenAI makes voice full-duplex with GPT-Live

On Jul 8, OpenAI introduced GPT-Live, a new generation of voice models built on a full-duplex architecture that can listen and speak at the same time — backchanneling ("mhmm"), interrupting, or staying quiet (OpenAI, TechCrunch). For harder queries it delegates to a frontier model behind the scenes (GPT-5.5 at launch) and brings the result back mid-conversation. Two versions roll out globally — GPT-Live-1 (default for paid) and GPT-Live-1 mini (free tier), positioning voice as the interface for agentic work (The AI Insider).

Full-duplex + frontier delegation is the template every voice-agent builder will now chase; if you ship voice, re-baseline latency and turn-taking expectations against GPT-Live before committing to a stack.

Google's cheap media stack: Nano Banana 2 Lite + Gemini Omni Flash

New to the feed (surfaced Jun 30, honestly not 24h): Google shipped Nano Banana 2 Lite — its fastest/cheapest image model (Gemini 3.1 Flash Lite Image), generating images in ~4 seconds at $0.034 / 1K images — and Gemini Omni Flash, the first of its any-to-any Omni family, which creates and edits video up to 10 seconds from text/image/video via conversational multi-turn editing at $0.10/sec (Google, TechCrunch). Both are live via AI Studio, the Gemini API and the Gemini Enterprise Agent Platform (Google Cloud).

Google is racing the cost floor down on generative media and folding it into its enterprise agent platform — if you generate images/video at scale, re-price against Nano Banana 2 Lite and Omni Flash before renewing incumbent tools.

IMPACT 5/10#4 RESEARCH · HEALTH

The first FDA-cleared patient-facing clinical LLM

The Friday vertical deep beat's health anchor: UpDoc (a Palo Alto company spun out of Stanford in 2023) announced Jun 25 the first FDA-cleared agentic clinical-AI platform using a patient-facing LLM — 510(k) K253281, a prescription device where adults with type-2 diabetes interact by voice or text for insulin-titration guidance (STAT, HLTH). Cleared via a drug-dose-calculator predicate and a Stanford titration trial; UpDoc also disclosed $18M seed and deployments at four health systems (McGuireWoods).

This reframes the debate from "can a patient-facing LLM be authorized" to "how narrowly must it be scoped" — the regulatory path for clinical-AI is opening one tightly-bounded indication at a time, not via broad chatbots.

The gate cleared its model — but the standing framework is now overdue

WHAT CHANGED The gate-then-clear cycle completed end-to-end: GPT-5.6 went from vetted-orgs preview to full GA (D1) after Commerce sign-off. But the voluntary covered-frontier framework — reported for the week of Jul 7 — has still not been announced, and that window has now passed. Meanwhile Meta shipped Muse Spark 1.1 (Hero) to US-only preview with no publicised gate.
The Jun 2 EO "Promoting Advanced AI Innovation and Security" grants up to 30 days of pre-release federal access (not a licence) and orders a classified cyber-capability benchmark to define "covered" models. Two clearances have now run (Fable 5, GPT-5.6), yet the standing rules that would generalise them remain unwritten (Nextgov/FCW).

The paradigm works case-by-case but hasn't been codified — the ~Aug 1 design deadline is the real test of whether "government reviews frontier releases" becomes standing policy or stays ad hoc.
