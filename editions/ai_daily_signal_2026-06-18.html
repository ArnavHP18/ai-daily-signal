Day 7: Anthropic's flagships stay dark — but Washington is now "working toward a deal," and Amodei shares a G7 table with Lutnick

Fable 5 and Mythos 5 remain disabled worldwide into Thursday, seven days after the Jun 12 Commerce directive barring foreign-national access. The new signal is movement: officials and Anthropic are reportedly working toward a deal to restore the models, with Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick holding regular calls and technical staff meeting nearly every day since Friday. The government's reasoning is now on the record: Lutnick said officials feared the models could be diverted to military-intelligence users in China, Russia or other countries of concern — the first named-official rationale, narrowing the earlier "uncorroborated China-access" caveat. And the optics sharpened Wednesday: at the G7 in Évian-les-Bains, CEO Dario Amodei and DeepMind's Demis Hassabis called for a US-led AI coalition even as Amodei negotiated his own models back online. Still no restoration timeline.

So what: a deal is being "worked," but seven days in there is still no date and a cabinet-level, on-record national-security rationale behind the shutoff. Keep your fallback live and price a real migration; a narrowing to nationality-gating is more likely than a clean switch-back.

Where it stands. As of this Thursday scan, both Fable 5 and Mythos 5 are still offline for all users worldwide — Day 7 of the blackout; every other Claude model remains available, with traffic routed to Opus 4.8. Anthropic says it hopes to restore access but has announced no timeline, and continues to dispute the directive while complying (Fortune, Anthropic).

Toward a deal. Per the Globe and Mail, Anthropic's technical staff have met with Commerce officials virtually every day since Friday, and Secretary Lutnick has held regular calls as he "works toward a deal"; the government is seeking assurances the models cannot be turned against the US, while Anthropic pushes for restoration. IBTimes frames the company as actively seeking terms. No agreement and no restoration date have been reached.

The rationale, now named. The earlier "China-access" concern — until now uncorroborated by any on-record official — has a face: Lutnick said he acted because officials feared Fable 5 and Mythos 5 could be diverted to military-intelligence users in China, Russia or other countries of concern. Foreign Policy earlier placed Treasury's Scott Bessent and Defense's Pete Hegseth among the principals; the Amazon-jailbreak account (Axios) remains the proximate trigger. Anthropic disputes that a narrow potential jailbreak justifies recalling a model deployed to hundreds of millions.

The G7 backdrop. At the Évian summit, Amodei and Hassabis called for a US-led AI coalition — structured access to frontier models, China-excluding trade in chips and critical components, and cooperation on cyber, bio and intelligence risk; Sam Altman also attended, alongside Trump, Lutnick, Bessent and Rubio (TNW, Fast Company). The juxtaposition is stark: the CEO whose models the US just took offline spent Wednesday arguing for a tighter Western AI bloc.

The defenders' revolt grows. A now 76-signatory open letter — Alex Stamos, Casey Ellis, Jon Callas and Katie Moussouris among them — urges Washington to lift the curbs, arguing the models are "not uniquely good" at offensive security and that China's are "only months behind" (Business Standard, iTnews). The EU Commission separately warned the controls "should not be discriminatory" toward European users (Euronews).

Watch next. Whether the daily talks yield a restoration date or a narrowing from a global shutoff to nationality gating; whether the G7 coalition idea gains signatories; and whether a parallel directive touches OpenAI or Google. Commentators keep treating the episode as a live test of a missing government AI-safety playbook (Tech Policy Press, Lawfare).

IMPACT 6/10#9 POLICY · EU

EU clock: 45 days to Aug 2 transparency + GPAI enforcement

Near steady state, calendar ticking. From Aug 2 the AI Act brings Article 50 transparency duties (disclosure when users interact with AI; labelling of synthetic media) plus the start of the Commission's GPAI enforcement powers — documentation/information requests, model evaluations, recall/withdrawal measures, and fines up to the greater of €15M or 3% of global turnover (Latham). The GPAI Code of Practice (transparency, copyright, safety & security) is the practical compliance tool; the safety chapter binds only the ~5–15 systemic-risk providers above 10^25 FLOP. ~24 firms (Anthropic, Google, Microsoft, Mistral, OpenAI and others) have signed. A separate Digital Omnibus postponement of some high-risk obligations is provisionally agreed but not yet formally adopted — Aug 2 transparency and GPAI enforcement still stand.

The Aug 2 enforcement switch is 45 days out — if your synthetic-media labelling and Article 50 transparency workstreams aren't staffed, they're late.
