The model the whole saga was gated on ships today — GPT-5.6 goes broadly available

The thread this briefing has carried since June 11 reaches its decisive event. On Jul 8 OpenAI announced that GPT-5.6 Sol, Terra and Luna — held since the Jun 26 preview to roughly 20 government-vetted organisations — are expanding to broad, global availability, with multiple trade outlets putting the wide launch at today, Jul 9 (OpenAI, Nextgov/FCW). The move follows the US Department of Commerce clearing the broad release after the government's cyber-capability safety review — Sol is OpenAI's most capable model yet for cyber tasks, which is precisely why it was gated (AI Weekly, Euronews). Yesterday this was a prediction-market-implied date (edition 026); today it is company-confirmed. Terra reportedly matches GPT-5.5 at ~2× lower cost, Luna is the fast/cheap tier, and Sol is serving on Cerebras at up to ~750 tokens/sec (TechTimes).

So what: the first model to clear the new US pre-release review process is now shipping to everyone — the gate held, and then opened. If you paused a migration waiting on GPT-5.6 (or lost cheap Fable 5 access, New-adjacent below), the eval window is open today. But note what has not shipped: OpenAI describes this as "expanding preview access globally," and there is still no full public system card at broad launch — treat the rollout as staged, not an instantaneous flip.

What happened. OpenAI said on Jul 8 that it is expanding preview access to GPT-5.6 globally after "working alongside government partners for safety evaluations," moving the family from the ~20 trusted-partner organisations that had access since the Jun 26 preview to broad availability through the API, Codex and ChatGPT. Trade coverage and prediction markets converge on today, Jul 9, as the wide-launch date — the exact date this briefing flagged on the Deadline Radar yesterday as "market-implied, not OpenAI-confirmed." It is now confirmed by the company's own announcement.

Why it's the lead. This is the resolution of the single longest-running frontier-model thread in the feed, and it is the first live test of the release paradigm the US built in June: a model deemed capable enough to warrant a government cyber-capability review, gated to vetted partners, then cleared for broad release by Commerce. The breadth of who must act is wide — every enterprise, developer and Codex user waiting on Sol/Terra/Luna, plus every competitor now watching how a government-reviewed launch actually lands. It also converges with three other threads today: the US voluntary covered-frontier framework (Developing), Google's own FARO proposal (New), and the Fable 5 pricing squeeze (Developing) that leaves buyers hunting for a cheaper flagship.

The caveats — read them. "Expanding preview access globally" is not the same as a clean GA flip; expect staged rollout and capacity throttling of the kind that has hit both OpenAI and Anthropic this summer. There is a preview system card on OpenAI's Deployment Safety Hub, but no full public system card accompanied the broad-launch announcement — METR had earlier flagged risk on Sol. Source outlets also disagree on the exact weekday label (some say "Thursday," one aggregator wrote Jul 10); the concrete, cross-checked facts are the Jul 8 announcement and the "this week / Jul 9" broad-launch window.

Watch next. Whether a full system card publishes with GA; real-world benchmarks on Sol's coding/cyber claims versus GPT-5.5, Fable 5 and GLM-5.2; pricing for the broad tier (Terra's "2× cheaper than GPT-5.5" framing); and whether the White House formally announces the voluntary covered-frontier framework this week, turning GPT-5.6's informal gate-then-clear cycle into standing policy.

IMPACT 6/10#1 HARDWARE · MEMORY

The AI memory crunch gets a courtroom: DRAM price-fixing class action

The Thursday deep beat's anchor: Samsung, SK Hynix and Micron — together ~90% of the DRAM market — face a US federal class action (filed Jun 25, 14 individuals + 3 small PC businesses) alleging they colluded under Sherman Act §1 to restrict consumer memory supply while steering wafers to AI's high-margin HBM. Plaintiffs claim prices spiked as much as 700%, dubbing it the "RAMpocalypse" (Tom's Hardware, Notebookcheck, Yahoo Finance). The complaint says all three began vetting buyers and policing orders in January; the makers themselves warn AI-driven shortages may last to 2027. Allegations are unproven — but the trio ran a DOJ-prosecuted DRAM cartel in 1998–2002.

The compute story is no longer just GPUs — memory is the binding constraint, and its economics are now a legal and consumer-politics risk. Budget for elevated DRAM/DDR5 through 2026–27 and expect antitrust scrutiny to shadow the HBM boom.

IMPACT 5/10#2 POLICY · US

Google pitches a frontier regulator as the labs enter federal review

On Jul 7, Google published "A Pragmatic Approach to AI Governance in America", proposing an industry-funded Frontier AI Regulatory Organisation (FARO) that would operate under federal oversight and set scientific benchmarks for frontier capabilities — especially cybersecurity and CBRN risk — plus standards for incident reporting and transparency (Google (white paper), Forbes). For widely-deployed AI, Google argues Washington should lean on existing law and focus on real-world harms, not "micromanaging development" (Digital Watch). It lands as three of four US frontier labs sit inside White House review in a single release cycle (Hero, Developing).

The labs are now writing the regulator's job description — watch whether a FARO-style benchmarking body gets folded into the White House's imminent voluntary framework, since it would define who faces pre-release review.

IMPACT 5/10#3 POLICY · INDIA

India signals a graded, risk-based AI law — with emergency shut-off powers

An official told The Economic Times (Jul 6) that India is weighing a graded, risk-based AI law: chatbots, productivity tools and recommenders would sit in a low-risk band with minimal regulation, while high-risk uses in banking, finance, health and critical infrastructure would carry heavier obligations (StartupFeed, White & Case (India tracker)). Notably, officials say the government has weighed powers to order dangerous AI systems switched off or to compel disclosure of technical details in an emergency — though no draft Bill, consultation paper or timeline exists yet. It sits atop the IT-Rules synthetic-content labelling already in force.

India is moving toward the EU's risk-tiering logic without (yet) the EU's timelines — if you deploy AI into Indian banking/health/finance, start mapping which of your systems would land in a "high-risk" band before a draft appears.

IMPACT 5/10#4 ENTERPRISE · FINANCE

The named-bank agentic wave: Goldman tests Claude agents; Oracle ships hundreds

Financial services — the sector furthest into production agents (~47% of banking/insurance firms run at least one) — is naming names. The applied-AI frontier is regulated back-office finance, not chatbots — if your competitor automates reconciliation and onboarding, the cost floor for the whole function drops; watch which pilots convert to production.
