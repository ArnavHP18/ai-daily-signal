China just trained a trillion-parameter frontier coder with zero US silicon — and open-sourced it

On June 30, Meituan open-sourced LongCat-2.0: a 1.6-trillion-parameter Mixture-of-Experts model (~33–56B active per token, 1M-token context) pitched at agentic coding and reportedly already topping OpenRouter. The strategic detail isn't the size — it's the substrate. Meituan says the entire run, pre-training and inference, happened from scratch on a ~50,000-card domestic Ascend cluster with in-house parallelism and the HCCL library — no Nvidia H100s, no AMD MI300X, no US hardware at all (VentureBeat, Crypto Briefing). The weights are live on Hugging Face under meituan-longcat. For 25 editions this feed has tracked Washington gating frontier models on export-control grounds; LongCat-2.0 is the counter-move made concrete — a self-hostable, near-frontier coder produced by a Chinese consumer-tech giant on a fully sovereign compute stack.

So what: the export-control lever assumes the frontier needs US chips to be built. A trillion-scale model trained end-to-end on Ascend says that assumption now has an expiry date — pull LongCat-2.0 into your coding-eval harness this week and price a self-hosted, export-immune fallback before the question is forced on you.

What shipped. LongCat-2.0 (released Jun 30, MIT-licensed) is a 1.6T-parameter MoE that dynamically activates ~33–56B parameters per token, with a 1M-token context window sized to hold an entire large codebase. Its agentic-coding scores are placed in the neighbourhood of GPT-5.5 / Gemini 3.1 Pro / Claude Opus 4.6, and it had reportedly been quietly leading OpenRouter's usage tables before the formal open-source drop (VentureBeat). It nearly tripled the parameter count of its predecessor in under a year.

Why it's the lead. The consequential claim is the compute sovereignty. Meituan states the full training run executed on a ~50,000-card Ascend 910-class domestic cluster using its own parallelism tweaks and Huawei's HCCL collective library — no US accelerators in the loop. If accurate, it is the first publicly documented trillion-scale model trained end-to-end without US silicon, which directly stress-tests the theory of the American export-control regime this briefing has tracked since edition 003.

The caveats. The coding-parity claims rest on OpenRouter usage and vendor framing, not an independent SWE-Bench / Terminal-Bench re-run in this scan. "Trained entirely on Chinese chips" is Meituan's own account; the exact cluster composition and any US-designed IP in the toolchain are not independently audited here. Self-hosting a 1.6T MoE is a real infrastructure cost, and MIT-licensed weights do not neutralise the agent-security surface tracked below.

Watch next. Independent benchmark re-runs; whether Western open-weight efforts (Llama, Mistral, gpt-oss) or GLM/DeepSeek respond; and whether the "no US silicon" training claim survives third-party scrutiny — the entire export-control thesis turns on it.
