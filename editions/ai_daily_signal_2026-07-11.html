The product, not the model: OpenAI puts an agent in front of every ChatGPT user

On Jul 9, OpenAI merged its Codex coding app into a single unified ChatGPT desktop app (Mac + Windows) and launched ChatGPT Work — an agent that "takes an outcome, gathers information across your apps and workflows, and stays with complex projects for hours," returning finished sheets, slides, docs and shareable web apps (Neowin, Crypto Briefing). The new app has Chat, Work and Codex in one place — and, crucially, all three are available on every plan, including Free (TechTimes). The old desktop app becomes "ChatGPT Classic." It lands two days after Anthropic took Claude Cowork to mobile and web (New #2) — the two leading labs shipping the same move within 48 hours: the agent, not the model, as the thing you actually buy.

So what: this is a distribution event, not a benchmark. Putting a project-scale agent (powered by GPT-5.6) in front of every free ChatGPT user — and collapsing the coding tool into the same surface — resets what "an AI assistant" means for hundreds of millions of people and pressures every standalone copilot and agent startup. Evaluate ChatGPT Work against your real multi-step workflows, but note it's a consolidation of existing pieces (GPT-5.6 + Codex + Work), not a new capability tier.

Grok 4.5 goes public — and finally gets benchmarked

WHAT CHANGED Yesterday this was a single-aggregator rumor; now it's confirmed. Grok 4.5 went public (Jul 8) as a cheap, Cursor-trained coding model, and its first days in the wild produced independent benchmarks: #4 on the Artificial Analysis Intelligence Index (score 54), behind Fable 5 (#1), GPT-5.5 (#2) and Opus 4.8 (#3), plus a token-efficiency claim that changes the cost math for high-volume agentic workloads.

A cheap #4 model with a real token-efficiency edge is a genuine option for cost-sensitive, high-volume agent runs — but weigh the recurring bias controversy and the missing Grok 5 before betting a stack on xAI.

IMPACT 5/10D3 POLICY · INDIA

India's AI-jobs paradox: hiring surges as a talent chasm opens

WHAT CHANGED The Saturday India-jobs beat sharpens with fresh Naukri data (Jul 3): AI-specific hiring rose 16% even as overall IT hiring fell ~3% YoY; AI roles are now 16% of all vacancies (2.9% in Jan 2023). But a NASSCOM/McKinsey/NITI Aayog estimate warns India could face a 14-lakh (1.4M) AI-talent shortfall by end-2026 without rapid upskilling.
The paradox: India is second only to the US in H1-2026 tech layoffs (7.16% of the global total), concentrated in EdTech and FinTech, even as AI/data becomes the fastest-growing function and drives ~64% of new GCC roles (ETV Bharat, CIO&Leader, Outlook Business).

India's AI labor market is bifurcating fast: routine IT roles shrink while specialist AI demand outstrips supply — the reskilling gap, not headcount, is now the binding constraint on India's AI-services growth story.

Whether "government reviews frontier releases" becomes standing policy or stays case-by-case will be decided in the next three weeks — the single most consequential open question for how US models ship.
