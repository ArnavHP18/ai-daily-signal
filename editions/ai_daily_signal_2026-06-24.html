SpaceX rents its rocket-money data center to an AI lab — and Nvidia is on both sides of the trade

SpaceX has signed a compute deal worth up to $6.3 billion with Reflection AI, an open-source AI lab, renting out capacity at its Colossus 2 data center near Memphis (TechCrunch, CNBC). The terms: $150 million a month for access to Nvidia's latest GB300 systems, payments beginning Jul 1, 2026 and running through 2029, with a 90-day exit after the first three months. It is smaller than SpaceX's reported compute deals with Anthropic (~$1.25B/mo) and Google (~$920M/mo) — but the structure is the story: Nvidia is an investor in Reflection (~$800M last year) and the chip vendor, so it helps finance a customer that then pays SpaceX to run Nvidia silicon (TFN). It also confirms SpaceX is now renting out Colossus 2 — the cluster many assumed was reserved for Grok — turning Elon Musk's merged AI-rocket entity into a compute landlord even as its own xAI competes for those GPUs.

So what: the AI build-out's circular financing (chipmaker → lab → cloud → back to the chipmaker) is now explicit and dated — if you're modeling compute supply or SPCX/Nvidia exposure, price in that frontier GPUs are being sold as a financialized, sub-leased asset, not just a capex line.

What was signed. SpaceX agreed to supply Reflection AI with compute capacity at its Colossus 2 facility near Memphis, Tennessee. Why it's the lead. Three threads converge. (1) SpaceX as landlord: having rented out Colossus 1 over its "mish-mash" of GPUs, SpaceX is now renting Colossus 2 too — raising questions about how much capacity is left for Grok. (2) Circular financing: Nvidia invested ~$800M in Reflection last year, so the chipmaker is funding the buyer that runs its chips on SpaceX's floor. (3) Open-source gets frontier compute: Reflection is an open-source lab, so frontier-scale GPUs are flowing to an open-weight effort just as the US restricts a closed US lab's models abroad (today's Developing threads).

The context. The deal lands against a 2026 data-center capex outlook now above $1 trillion, with the five largest US hyperscalers alone committing ~$660–690B (BloombergNEF). Reflection's ~$150M/mo is modest beside Anthropic's and Google's SpaceX deals, but it widens the renter base for a single private cluster.

Watch next. Whether the first payment lands on Jul 1 as scheduled; how SpaceX balances rented capacity against xAI/Grok's own training needs; and whether more open-source labs follow Reflection onto Colossus — making SpaceX a neutral compute utility rather than just xAI's host.

Europe picks the Domyn-led EUROPA consortium to build a sovereign open frontier model

The European Commission named EUROPA — a consortium led by Italian firm Domyn — winner of its Frontier AI Grand Challenge (Jun 19), tasked with building an open-source model exceeding 400B parameters trained natively across all 24 official EU languages (EC Digital Strategy, heise). The project gets up to 2.5% of all EuroHPC supercomputing capacity for a year — one of the EU's largest-ever AI compute allocations — under the Apply AI Strategy.

Europe is funding a sovereign, open alternative precisely as the US shows it can switch a closed flagship off abroad — a direct read-through for buyers who need data-residency and export-proof models.

IMPACT 8/10D3 POLICY · SECURITY

Agent-security reckoning gets a name: "Comment and Control" hits three vendors at once

WHAT CHANGED From trajectory to a worked cross-vendor exploit: Johns Hopkins researchers showed a single malicious GitHub PR title ("Comment and Control") made Anthropic's Claude Code Security Review, Google's Gemini CLI Action and GitHub's Copilot Agent exfiltrate secrets — including an ANTHROPIC_API_KEY and GITHUB_TOKEN — via PR comments and Actions logs.
The PR title flows unsanitized into the agent's prompt context, breaking it and inserting a new instruction (VentureBeat, OODAloop). One vendor's system card had predicted exactly this — yet shipped without enforcement. It reinforces OWASP's framing of prompt injection as the #1 agentic threat (reported +340% YoY) and the "lethal trifecta" of private data + untrusted input + external comms (Help Net, CSO).

If you run coding agents in CI, the trifecta is already in your pipeline — sanitize untrusted fields, scope tokens, and never let a PR title reach an agent's context unfiltered.
