■ Today's Lead · New · Policy · Day 1 of the US-state-reg arc

America's first comprehensive state AI law was supposed to take effect today. It won't.

The Colorado AI Act (SB 205, 2024) — the most far-reaching state AI statute in the US, a risk-based regime governing "consequential decisions" in employment, housing, healthcare, lending and education — was set to become enforceable today, June 30 2026. Instead, on May 14 Governor Polis signed SB 189, which delays the effective date to January 1 2027 and guts the core of the law: it eliminates the developer/deployer duty of care against algorithmic discrimination, drops mandatory risk-management programs and impact assessments, and removes most Attorney-General reporting — narrowing the surviving obligations to disclosure and transparency around certain automated-decision tools (Littler, Troutman). Commentators read it as an explicit pivot away from the EU model, with a White House call-out in the background (Hunton). The contrast with the same day's other beats is the story: as the EU's binding Article 50 + GPAI enforcement switches on Aug 2 (Developing), the first serious US attempt at horizontal AI law has been pared back to notices — the deregulatory current that also runs through the Jun 2 federal EO.

So what: if your 2026 compliance calendar had a "Colorado go-live" milestone for today, delete it — but don't bank the savings. The duty-of-care framework is deferred to Jan 1 2027, the EU's harder obligations still bite Aug 2, and the US picture is now disclosure-first, not risk-first; re-baseline your AI-governance roadmap to the surviving transparency duties and the EU clock.

What the original law did. Enacted in 2024, the Colorado AI Act was the first US state law to impose EU-style, risk-based obligations on "high-risk" AI systems used in consequential decisions — a duty of reasonable care to protect against algorithmic discrimination, risk-management programs, impact assessments, consumer notices and AG reporting. Its effective date had already been pushed (to June 30 2026) amid implementation concern.

What SB 189 changed. Signed May 14 2026, it postpones the effective date again — to Jan 1 2027 — and substantially reduces obligations: gone are the duty of care, the risk-management mandate and the impact-assessment requirement; what remains centers on disclosure/transparency for certain automated-decision technologies (Littler, Troutman).

Why it's the lead. Today was the date the entire US compliance community had circled. The fact that it arrives as a non-event — and as a deliberate retreat from the risk-based design — is the signal: state-level horizontal AI regulation in the US is bending toward disclosure-only, while the binding obligations now live almost entirely on the EU side (Aug 2) and inside the federal executive's authorization-based access regime (the Anthropic/OpenAI export thread).

Caveats & watch next. SB 189 is law, but rulemaking and the narrowed transparency duties still have to be operationalized before Jan 1 2027; other states (and a federal preemption push) are moving in parallel. Watch whether any state holds the line on a duty-of-care model, and how the EU's Aug 2 enforcement reshapes multinationals' baselines regardless of the US retreat.
