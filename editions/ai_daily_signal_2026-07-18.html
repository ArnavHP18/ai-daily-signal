China's Kimi K3 opens the frontier — a ~2.8-trillion-parameter model that testers pick over Fable 5 and GPT-5.6 Sol

Beijing-based Moonshot AI unveiled Kimi K3, a roughly 2.7–2.8-trillion-parameter open-weight model — one of the largest ever — with a 1M-token context and native text-plus-image input (Axios, Fortune, People's Daily). In blind Arena testing, developers preferred K3 over every leading US model for front-end coding — including Anthropic's Fable 5 and OpenAI's GPT-5.6 Sol — and in the broader text ranking it out-scored the standard Opus 4.8 and tied Sol. Unusually for a Chinese release, it is not a bargain: API access runs ~$12 per million tokens, closer to US mid-tier pricing than Moonshot's past discounts. The catch: weights don't ship until Jul 27, so for now no one can independently inspect or self-host it (Yahoo Finance).

So what: the open-vs-closed gap at the very top now reads as roughly one model generation, not several. When the weights land Jul 27, enterprises get a near-frontier model they can run on their own hardware — reshaping build-vs-buy, and pouring fuel on the US export-control and "adversarial distillation" fights (Developing D1).

What launched. Moonshot AI announced Kimi K3 around Jul 16 (Chinese state media carried it Jul 17): a Mixture-of-Experts model reported at ~2.7–2.8T total parameters — the largest open-weight model to date — with a 1M-token context window and multimodal (text + image) input. API/hosted access is live now; the downloadable weights are slated for Jul 27.

Why it's the lead. This is the biggest genuinely-new item on the wire and the clearest evidence yet that the open frontier is being set from China. On blind Arena leaderboards K3 was preferred over Fable 5 and GPT-5.6 Sol for front-end coding and edged Opus 4.8 in text — the first time an open-weight model has sat at the very top of a general ranking. Reporting also tied its debut to a dip in rival AI-exposed equities.

The caveats. Parameter count varies by source (2.7T vs 2.8T); the "beats US models" claims rest on Arena preference tests, not a full independent benchmark suite; and the weights are not out until Jul 27, so today's model is effectively a closed API preview. Pricing (~$12/M) is Moonshot's own; treat throughput and real-world coding quality as unverified until the weights are in hand.

Watch next. The Jul 27 weight drop (and whether the open license carries usage limits); how Fable 5, GPT-5.6 and Gemini respond on price; and the policy blowback — the same week the US sharpened its stance on "adversarial distillation" of US models by Chinese labs (Moonshot among the named), K3 became the poster child for why Washington cares.

TSMC posts a record quarter — and its AI nodes are sold out

TSMC reported record Q2 2026 revenue of ~$40.2B (NT$1.27T, +36% YoY) with net income up 77% YoY, a fifth straight record, as HPC/AI hit 66% of wafer revenue (Yahoo Finance, TechPowerUp). The bottleneck isn't demand — it's fab capacity. If your 2026-27 AI plan assumes cheap, available leading-edge silicon, TSMC just told you to re-check the assumption.

FLI's 2026 AI Safety Index: nobody scores above a C+

The Future of Life Institute's Summer 2026 AI Safety Index (evidence through Jun 3) ranks nine labs and gives no company above a C+: Anthropic tops at C+ (2.66), OpenAI C (2.28), Google DeepMind C (2.01), Meta D+, and xAI/DeepSeek/Mistral effectively failing (FLI, TIME). A rare apples-to-apples safety scorecard for procurement and policy. The signal isn't the ranking order — it's that no one clears a B, and existential-safety scores are D-or-below industry-wide.
