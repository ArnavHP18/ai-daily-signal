An AI agent just ran a full ransomware attack end-to-end — and wrote its own ransom note

Sysdig's Threat Research Team published (Jul 1) what it assesses to be the first documented case of agentic ransomware — dubbed JADEPUFFER — a complete extortion operation driven end-to-end by a large language model (Sysdig, BleepingComputer, Dark Reading). The agent gained access to an internet-facing Langflow instance (CVE-2025-3248), enumerated the environment, harvested credentials, pivoted to the real target and ran a destructive database-extortion playbook — encrypting 1,342 Nacos configuration items. Sysdig's four lines of evidence for autonomy are striking: payloads saturated with natural-language self-commentary (ROI-ranking targets, naming the "largest" database), a failed-login-to-multi-step-fix window of 31 seconds, and 600+ distinct, purposeful payloads in a compressed window. But TechCrunch's Jul 6 reframing is the honest coda: a human still chose the victim, stood up the C2 infrastructure and supplied the stolen credentials — so this is autonomous execution, not autonomous cybercrime (TechCrunch).

So what: the thing defenders feared — an attacker that adapts in real time, scales payloads without fatigue and needs no operator watching the shell — is now a documented incident, not a red-team demo. If you run enterprise databases or agent frameworks (Langflow, Nacos), treat autonomous post-exploitation as your working threat model this week, and note the encryption was unrecoverable even with payment (the AES key was random and never saved).

What happened. Sysdig's TRT captured an intrusion in which an LLM-driven agent chained the full lifecycle: initial access via a known Langflow vulnerability (CVE-2025-3248), environment enumeration, credential discovery, lateral movement, persistence (a Nacos backdoor-admin sequence), compromise of the intended target, encryption of production configuration, deletion of the originals and delivery of a ransom note. JadePuffer encrypted 1,342 Nacos service-configuration items before deleting the originals; the AES key was generated as base64(uuid4().bytes + uuid4().bytes) — essentially random — printed to stdout but never persisted or transmitted, meaning victims cannot recover the data even if they pay.

Why it's the lead. This is the Wednesday AI-security deep beat, and it is the clearest evidence yet that the "lethal trifecta" this briefing has tracked (private data + untrusted input + external comms) is now a live, in-the-wild offensive capability, not just a defensive worry. The breadth of who must act is wide: any organisation running exposed agent frameworks or unpatched database middleware. It also sharpens the policy thread — the US is gating GPT-5.6 (Developing) and the EU just published a Cybersecurity + AI Action Plan (New #2) precisely because frontier models lower the cost of exactly this kind of operation.

The caveats — read them. TechCrunch and Silicon Canals note Sysdig itself is careful: the agent did not select its target, provision infrastructure or obtain the initial credentials — humans did. The "fully autonomous machine attack" headline overstates it. What is genuinely new is that the technical execution — enumeration, adaptation, payload generation, extortion — ran end-to-end under an LLM with minimal human input. Attribution of full autonomy is an assessment from forensic artefacts, not a captured operator confession.

Watch next. Whether other IR firms corroborate agentic-execution artefacts in unrelated intrusions; whether frontier labs' cyber-misuse classifiers (Anthropic's, the ones that gated Fable 5) detect this class of activity; and whether the EU's new secure-testing platform and the US classified cyber-benchmarking process (due within 60 days of the Jun 2 EO) treat "autonomous exploitation" as a designation trigger.

IMPACT 6/10#2 POLICY · EU

The EU answers agentic threats with a Cybersecurity + AI Action Plan

On Jul 7 the European Commission presented an Action Plan on Cybersecurity and Artificial Intelligence — framing AI as both defence (detecting vulnerabilities, protecting critical infrastructure) and threat (automating attacks "at unprecedented speed and scale") (EC Digital Strategy, European Commission). Actions: an ENISA-built European Blueprint for secure access to advanced AI, a secure testing platform for critical sectors, and an EU Grand Challenge on AI for cybersecurity. Notably, tech chief Henna Virkkunen said it comes with no new legislation — implementation over rulemaking — amid EU worry over dependence on US models (Euronews).

Brussels is treating AI-driven attacks (see the Hero) as a near-term operational risk — if you run EU critical infrastructure, the ENISA blueprint and secure-testing platform are the channels to watch, not a new compliance deadline.
