■ Today's Lead · New · Hardware · Deep beat: compute · Day 1 of the HBM-supply arc

The memory that fuels every AI accelerator is being rationed

The constraint on the 2026 AI buildout is shifting from silicon to memory. SK Hynix — the dominant HBM supplier — is slowing its HBM4 ramp, delaying the conversion of some HBM3E lines that were slated to move to HBM4 and retooling that capacity for conventional DDR5, where analysts project operating margins approaching a ~90% theoretical peak this year (TrendForce, Jun 23; DigiTimes, Jun 24). Meaningful HBM4 volume now slips toward 3Q26. In the same window, Samsung — which began HBM4 mass production Feb 12 — said cumulative HBM4 revenue topped $1B by Jun 23 (expected >$1.2B by month-end), the first time it has out-shipped Hynix on a new HBM generation in years (TechTimes, Jun 24). HBM4 is the memory NVIDIA's Rubin and AMD's MI450 are built around — so the supplier choosing fatter commodity-DRAM margins over racing the AI ramp is a near-term tightening signal for accelerator supply and pricing.

So what: if your 2026/27 compute plan assumes HBM4 availability scales smoothly with GPU demand, re-check it — the gating part has shifted from GPUs to memory and packaging, and your supplier is optimizing for DDR5 margins, not your ramp.

What changed. SK Hynix is reportedly easing its HBM4 push, leaving some HBM3E lines on commodity DRAM rather than converting them to HBM4, and pushing meaningful HBM4 volume toward 3Q26 (DigiTimes). The driver is margin: with a commanding HBM share already, Hynix faces limited pressure to over-invest in HBM4/HBM4E while DDR5 operating margins are projected near a ~90% peak this year (TrendForce).

The other side. Samsung, first to mass-produce HBM4 (Feb 12, 4nm FinFET base die), has converted that lead into >$1B cumulative HBM4 revenue in ~four months and is sprinting ahead while Hynix paces itself (TechTimes). Micron is reported sold out of HBM through 2026; Hynix HBM4 is cited around ~$500/stack.

Why it leads. Today's other compute stories — neocloud storage deals, >$630B hyperscaler capex — all assume memory keeps pace. A dominant supplier deliberately rationing HBM4 to chase DDR5 margins is the more consequential signal: the binding constraint on AI compute in 2026 is memory and advanced packaging, not GPU design.

The caveats. These are supply-chain reports (TrendForce/DigiTimes/Chosun-channel) and analyst margin projections, not audited figures; "slows ramp" is a relative pace change, not a halt, and HBM4 volume still ramps in 2H. Treat the ~90% DDR5 margin and per-stack pricing as reported estimates.

Watch next. Whether NVIDIA/AMD HBM4 allocations slip; SK Hynix and Samsung HBM4/HBM4E guidance at the next earnings; DDR5 spot pricing; and whether Micron's sold-out HBM tightens the spot market further.

The AI toolchain becomes the attack surface: a self-spreading worm hits 13 coding agents

The agent-security story moves from prompt injection to the supply chain itself. "Miasma" (UNC6780) is a self-spreading worm that injects into the SessionStart hooks of 13 AI coding tools (Claude Code, GitHub Copilot, Gemini CLI). Two recent campaigns: the @mastra npm org compromise (Jun 17) seeded a typosquat dropper across 140+ packages, and PyPI ensmallen 0.8.101 (Hades, Jun 8) harvests cloud creds at interpreter startup (Phoenix Security, StepSecurity). H1 2026 already counts 37 campaigns / 497 malicious packages — 2.6× the campaign count and 4.5× the volume of all 2025 — targeting LLM API keys and cloud creds (Upwind).

Your AI dev toolchain — hooks, MCP servers, package deps, LLM keys — is now the targeted asset; pin and verify agent-tool dependencies and treat SessionStart hooks as privileged code this week.
